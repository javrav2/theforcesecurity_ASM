## The State of OSINT in 2026

The OSINT landscape has exploded. Between the proliferation of data brokers, the growth of internet-connected devices, and the rise of AI-assisted analysis tools, the amount of publicly available intelligence has never been greater.

For defenders, this is both an opportunity and a threat. The same tools that help red teamers and threat intelligence analysts profile targets are available to adversaries profiling your organization.

Here's our current toolkit.

## Passive Reconnaissance

### Shodan

Shodan remains indispensable. Its database of internet-connected devices, updated continuously, gives you a real-time view of what services are exposed. In 2026, Shodan's AI features can now summarize exposure patterns and flag trending vulnerabilities across your monitored IP ranges.

**Best for:** External attack surface visibility, IoT exposure, banner grabbing at scale  
**Pricing:** Freemium (personal plan: $49/mo)  
**Verdict:** ★★★★★

### Censys

Censys is Shodan's closest competitor and arguably more accurate for enterprise IP ranges. Its ASM platform has matured significantly and integrates well with SIEM/SOAR workflows.

**Best for:** Certificate transparency monitoring, ASM, enterprise exposure tracking  
**Pricing:** Freemium  
**Verdict:** ★★★★☆

### GreyNoise

GreyNoise filters out internet noise — scanners, crawlers, and mass-exploitation tools — helping you focus on targeted activity. If Shodan tells you *what* is exposed, GreyNoise tells you *who is scanning for it*.

**Best for:** SOC triage, reducing alert fatigue, threat hunting  
**Pricing:** Freemium  
**Verdict:** ★★★★★

## Domain and DNS Intelligence

### Subfinder

```bash
subfinder -d target.com -o subdomains.txt -all -recursive
```

The gold standard for passive subdomain enumeration. Fast, accurate, and integrates with 50+ data sources.

### amass

More configurable than Subfinder and supports active enumeration modes. Better for thorough engagements where speed is secondary to completeness.

### DNSx

Part of the ProjectDiscovery toolkit, DNSx resolves and validates discovered subdomains at scale with support for wildcard filtering.

## Certificate Transparency

Certificate transparency logs are a goldmine that many defenders underutilize:

```bash
# Query crt.sh for all certs issued to a domain
curl "https://crt.sh/?q=%.target.com&output=json" | jq -r '.[].name_value' | sort -u
```

Tools like **certstream** let you monitor CT logs in real-time — catching newly issued certificates for your domains (and subdomains you didn't know about) as they appear.

## Social and People Intelligence

### Maltego

Maltego's transform framework lets you connect the dots between domains, IPs, email addresses, social profiles, and corporate entities. The visualization makes complex relationship mapping intuitive.

**Best for:** Investigation workflows, APT attribution, third-party risk  
**Pricing:** Freemium (community) / Commercial  
**Verdict:** ★★★★☆

### theHarvester

A classic tool for gathering emails, subdomains, and hosts from public sources like search engines and LinkedIn. Simple but effective for initial target profiling.

## Code and Secrets Intelligence

### GitHub Dorking

Don't underestimate what's exposed on GitHub. Developers accidentally commit credentials, API keys, and internal architecture details constantly:

```
org:targetcompany filename:.env
org:targetcompany "DB_PASSWORD"
org:targetcompany "aws_access_key_id"
```

### TruffleHog

Scan git history for secrets at scale. Useful both offensively (finding leaked credentials) and defensively (auditing your own repos).

## Building an OSINT Workflow

The key is systematizing your approach. Our recommended workflow for external recon:

1. **Seed** — start with the root domain and known IP ranges
2. **Expand** — CT logs, passive DNS, WHOIS, ASN enumeration
3. **Resolve** — validate live assets with httpx
4. **Profile** — Shodan/Censys for service fingerprinting
5. **Analyze** — correlate findings, identify patterns
6. **Document** — everything in structured format for follow-up

The best OSINT practitioners think like investigators, not just tool operators.
